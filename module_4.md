# Module 4 — End-to-End Workflow
### Barts Health NHS × La Fosse — Microsoft 365 Data Skills Training

**Day 2 | 11:30–13:00 | Platform: Excel Online, PowerPoint Online, Microsoft Copilot**
**Three blocks: 45-minute scenario, 30-minute peer review, 15-minute action planning and close**

---

## Overview

Module 4 is different in character from everything that has come before it. There is no new content to learn, no formula to memorise, no framework being introduced for the first time. The purpose of this module is to find out whether the skills from Modules 1, 2, and 3 are genuinely embedded — and to give participants the experience of applying them together, under mild time pressure, on a realistic task.

The scenario is modelled on the kind of work participants already do or will be expected to do: a raw dataset, a defined audience, a brief, and a deadline. What they produce in forty-five minutes is less important than the decisions they make along the way — what they clean and what they leave, which chart type they choose and why, where they use Copilot and where they do not, and how they frame the finding for the person receiving it.

The debrief and peer review that follow are not formalities. They are where the learning from the scenario is consolidated — where participants articulate what worked, what they would do differently, and what they now understand about their own approach to data work that they did not before the session started.

---

## Where Module 4 Sits in the Data Lifecycle

```
Collection → Storage → Access → Cleaning → Analysis → Visualisation → Communication → Action
```

Module 4 spans the entire lifecycle from **Access** through to **Communication**. For the first time in the programme, participants are not being guided through a specific stage — they are managing the whole journey themselves, making their own judgements about sequencing, tool choice, and what matters most given the time available.

This is how data work actually happens in a busy NHS trust. There is rarely enough time to be thorough about everything. The skill being tested here is prioritisation: given forty-five minutes and a brief, what do you do first, what do you do well, and what do you accept will be imperfect?

---

## Learning Goals

By the end of this module, participants will be able to:

- Apply the full data workflow — from raw data to stakeholder-ready output — independently and without step-by-step guidance
- Make real-time judgements about when to use manual techniques and when to use Copilot
- Produce an output that is clear, accurate, and appropriately framed for the stated audience and purpose
- Articulate what they did well, what they would improve, and what they have learned about their own approach
- Identify one or two specific, concrete ways they will apply the programme's skills in their own role

---

## Main Areas Covered

- A timed end-to-end practical scenario using a Barts Health-specific dataset and brief
- Independent decision-making across data cleaning, analysis, visualisation, and communication
- Peer review using a structured feedback framework
- Group debrief: patterns across approaches, common decisions, and shared challenges
- Personal action planning: committing to behaviour change, not just skill acquisition

## Block Breakdown

---

### Block 1 — End-to-End Scenario
**11:30–12:15 | 45 minutes**

#### What we cover

Participants receive a scenario brief and a raw, anonymised Barts Health dataset. The brief specifies:

- **The dataset**: a realistic export from a Barts Health system (e.g. a monthly mandatory training completion report, a staffing absence summary, or a departmental spend tracker) containing deliberate data quality issues
- **The audience**: a named senior stakeholder role (e.g. a clinical director, a head of operations, or an executive sponsor) with a brief description of what they care about and what they will do with the output
- **The ask**: a specific deliverable — for example, a one-page summary slide in PowerPoint Online, or a short written briefing in Word Online — that presents the key finding and a recommendation
- **The time available**: forty-five minutes, which is enough to do the task well if decisions are made efficiently, and not enough to do everything perfectly

Participants work independently. The facilitator circulates to observe, ask questions about decisions being made, and offer support where someone is stuck — not to direct, but to keep the room moving.

Participants are expected to draw on all three previous modules in sequence:

1. **Access and clean the data** (Module 1): identify and resolve quality issues, structure the data as a Table, apply any necessary formulas or lookups
2. **Analyse and visualise** (Modules 1 and 2): identify the key finding the brief is asking about, build and format an appropriate chart, write an insight-led title
3. **Build the output and communicate** (Modules 2 and 3): structure the deliverable using the Context → Insight → Recommendation framework, use Copilot where it genuinely saves time, review and edit any AI-generated content before including it

Copilot use is encouraged where it adds speed — generating a formula, drafting slide narrative, producing a first-cut email — and participants are expected to apply the validation habits from Module 3 to everything Copilot produces.

#### Why this matters

There is a significant difference between being able to follow a worked example and being able to do the work independently. Every block in Modules 1, 2, and 3 has been guided: the task has been defined, the tools identified, the approach demonstrated. In real reporting work, none of that scaffolding exists. You open a file, you have a brief and a deadline, and you have to make the decisions yourself.

Module 4 creates a safe version of that experience. The stakes are low — this is a training session, not a board paper — but the conditions are real enough to surface the moments of uncertainty that participants will actually face back at their desks: not knowing which chart type to choose, not being sure whether a data quality issue is significant enough to fix or safe to leave, not knowing how to frame a finding for an audience they have limited context on.

Experiencing and navigating those moments of uncertainty — even imperfectly — is what makes the programme's skills durable. Skills practised in isolation tend to fade. Skills applied under mild pressure, and then reflected on, tend to stick.

#### Where you will use this in the data lifecycle

Module 4 spans the entire lifecycle, but the skill it is really developing is **prioritisation** — knowing which parts of the lifecycle to invest time in given the available resources, and accepting that a well-executed 80% is usually more valuable than an incomplete 100%.

At Barts Health, this applies every time a reporting deadline is tighter than the task deserves: a request for a directorate summary by end of day, a request to pull together figures for a meeting that is happening in an hour. The participants who perform well in those situations are not the ones who know the most formulas — they are the ones who can make fast, defensible decisions about where to spend their effort and where to accept imperfection.

### Block 2 — Peer Review & Group Debrief
**12:15–12:45 | 30 minutes**

#### What we cover

**Peer review (12:15–12:30):**

Participants share their outputs in pairs and give structured feedback using three questions:

1. **What is clear?** What does the output communicate well — what would a senior stakeholder understand immediately from looking at it?
2. **What is missing or unclear?** What would the stakeholder need to ask about, or what might they misunderstand without additional context?
3. **What would you do differently?** If you had this brief with the same data and the same time, what one change would you make to the output?

The feedback is constructive and specific — not general praise or general criticism, but observations tied to the output in front of them. Participants are practising the skill of critically evaluating a data communication product, which is a valuable capability in its own right.

**Group debrief (12:30–12:45):**

The facilitator leads a structured group reflection covering:

- What data quality issues did people find, and what did they decide to do with them? Were there differences in approach, and what drove them?
- What chart type did people choose, and did different people make different choices? What was the reasoning?
- Where did people use Copilot, and where did they not? What drove those decisions?
- Where did people get stuck, and what did they do when they got stuck?
- What would each person do differently if they had another forty-five minutes?

The debrief is not a performance review — it is a collective sense-making exercise. The goal is for participants to hear that other people made different decisions, understand the reasoning behind those decisions, and reflect on whether their own approach was well-suited to the brief or whether there is something they would adjust.

#### Why this matters

Feedback on your own work from a peer — someone who did the same task with the same data in the same time — is unusually instructive. They are not an expert telling you what you should have done; they are someone who faced the same decisions you faced and made some of them differently. Understanding where those differences came from, and whether the differences matter, is how people develop a more nuanced and confident approach to their own work.

The group debrief serves a different purpose: it surfaces the range of approaches taken across the room and gives the facilitator the opportunity to highlight the decisions that consistently produced better outcomes, and to address the places where the group collectively struggled. This is where the programme's concepts are reinforced not through instruction but through recognition — participants see the principles from Modules 1, 2, and 3 reflected in their own experience of the scenario.

#### Where you will use this in the data lifecycle

Peer review and reflection are habits rather than tools, and they sit at the end of the **Communication** stage — after an output has been produced and before it is sent or published. In practice at Barts Health:

- Before sharing a report with a directorate lead or senior manager, asking a colleague to read it and answer the three questions from the peer review framework takes five minutes and can catch errors, omissions, or framing issues that are invisible to the person who built it
- After a regular reporting cycle (monthly, quarterly), taking ten minutes to reflect on what worked and what to improve next time is a low-cost habit with a meaningful cumulative impact on reporting quality
- Within a team, sharing approaches to common reporting tasks — how do you usually clean this dataset? what chart type do you use for this metric? — builds shared standards and reduces the variation that occurs when everyone is working independently

### Block 3 — Action Planning & Close
**12:45–13:00 | 15 minutes**

#### What we cover

- Individual action planning: each participant writes down one or two specific, concrete changes they will make to how they approach data work in their role within the next thirty days
- The emphasis is on specificity — not "I will use Excel more" but "When I receive the monthly compliance export, I will run the cleaning checklist from Module 1 before building any summary formulas"
- Participants share their commitment with one other person in the room, creating a minimal but meaningful accountability structure
- Facilitator closes with a summary of the key themes from both days and a signpost to the post-session resources: the 30-day recap guides, challenge tasks, Copilot prompt library, and the 30-day check-in
- Participants leave with a clear idea of what the next step is — not just that they attended training, but what they are going to do differently on Monday morning

#### Why this matters

Training that ends without a commitment to action tends to fade. Participants return to their desks, their inboxes fill up, their existing habits reassert themselves, and the tools and frameworks from the session are accessed once or twice and then quietly dropped. This is not a failure of motivation — it is the normal response to new information that has not yet been connected to a specific context or trigger.

Action planning changes this by connecting the skills from the programme to a specific situation in a participant's actual work. The question is not "what did you learn?" but "what will you do differently, and when?" That specificity is what creates the bridge between training and behaviour change.

The thirty-day reinforcement resources — the challenge tasks, the prompt library, the check-in — are designed to maintain that connection over the period when it is most likely to weaken. The check-in in particular gives participants a specific moment to account for whether they followed through, and an opportunity to raise any barriers they have encountered.

#### Where you will use this in the data lifecycle

Action planning sits outside the data lifecycle in the conventional sense — it is the meta-skill that determines whether the data lifecycle is actually improved as a result of this programme. At Barts Health, the most common and impactful changes participants can commit to are:

- Running a first-look audit on any new dataset before doing any analytical work on it (Module 1, Block 1)
- Applying the cleaning checklist before building formulas or charts, and documenting any data quality issues found (Module 1, Block 2)
- Switching from VLOOKUP to XLOOKUP for any new formulas in shared reporting files (Module 1, Block 4)
- Writing insight-led chart titles on any visual included in a report for a senior stakeholder (Module 2, Block 2)
- Using the Context → Insight → Recommendation framework to structure any data communication, however brief (Module 2)
- Applying the five-component prompt structure when using Copilot for any task, rather than typing a short keyword prompt and hoping for the best (Module 3, Block 1)
- Running the validation checklist on any Copilot output before using or sharing it (Module 3, Block 4)

Each of these is a small, specific habit change. Together, they add up to a meaningfully different approach to data work — one that produces more reliable outputs, communicates more clearly to senior audiences, and makes better use of the tools available in Microsoft 365.

## Module Conclusion

Module 4 does not conclude with new knowledge — it concludes with a commitment. Participants have demonstrated that they can apply the programme's skills independently, have received feedback on their output from a peer, and have identified the specific moment in their own work where they will apply something they have learned.

The key principles from this module are:

- **Application is the test.** You do not fully know a skill until you have applied it under real conditions, without a worked example to follow. Module 4 creates that test in a safe environment.
- **Imperfect and complete is better than perfect and unfinished.** Under time pressure, prioritisation is the skill. A clear, correctly framed finding from clean data is worth more than a technically comprehensive report that was not finished.
- **Specific commitments beat general intentions.** "I will use XLOOKUP" does not change behaviour. "When I next open the compliance tracker, I will update the VLOOKUP in column F to use XLOOKUP" does.
- **Reflection closes the loop.** The peer review and debrief are not add-ons to the scenario — they are where the scenario's learning is consolidated. Applying a skill and then reflecting on how you applied it is how that skill becomes durable.

## Post-Programme: 30-Day Reinforcement

Following both sessions, participants receive structured resources to support the embedding of skills in their day-to-day roles:

- **Recap guides** for each module — concise one-page references for formulas, cleaning steps, chart type selection, prompting frameworks, and the narrative structure
- **Challenge tasks** — four short, practical exercises delivered over thirty days, each based on a realistic Barts Health scenario, designed to be completed in under thirty minutes using the skills from the programme
- **Copilot prompt library** — a curated set of ready-to-use prompt templates for the most common recurring tasks: monthly compliance summary emails, meeting action note formats, report narrative drafts, formula generation requests
- **30-day check-in** — a brief facilitator-led conversation to measure confidence and application, surface any barriers encountered, and identify whether any aspect of the programme needs revisiting

The goal of the reinforcement period is not to extend the training — it is to prevent the decay that normally follows it. Thirty days of structured, low-effort practice, connected to real work, is what converts a training experience into a durable change in how people work.
